# Supercritical Water and Geothermal Energy

## Background

A new bipartisan bill (the Hot Rock Act) proposes investing in **supercritical geothermal energy** — drilling deep enough below the Earth's surface that temperatures exceed 375 &deg;C, the point at which water enters a **supercritical state**. Supercritical fluids carry far more energy per unit mass than conventional steam, meaning each well could generate significantly more power.

## Part 3: Kinetics and the Arrhenius Equation

One challenge for supercritical geothermal wells is the corrosion of metal drilling equipment. The corrosion of iron by water can be described as:

$$\text{3 Fe(s) + 4 H}_2\text{O(g)} \rightarrow \text{Fe}_3\text{O}_4\text{(s) + 4 H}_2\text{(g)}$$

The activation energy for this corrosion reaction is approximately $E_a = 75 \text{ kJ/mol}$ and the rate constant at 25 &deg;C is $k_{25} = 1.2 \times 10^{-8} \text{ s}^{-1}$.

**3a.** Use the Arrhenius equation to calculate the rate constant at 400 &deg;C (a typical supercritical geothermal temperature). The Arrhenius equation is:

$$\ln\left(\frac{k_2}{k_1}\right) = \frac{E_a}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2}\right)$$

where $R = 8.314 \text{ J/(mol·K)}$.

(*Remember to convert temperatures to Kelvin.*)

**3b.** By what factor does the rate constant increase when going from 25 &deg;C to 400 &deg;C? What does this imply about the rate of corrosion in supercritical geothermal wells compared to surface conditions?

**3c.** The bill proposes research into "high-temperature completions" for wells. Based on your answer to 3b, explain why developing corrosion-resistant materials is critical for supercritical geothermal energy.
